Does Money Have Value?

I studied anthropology and archaeology in my undergrad. I believe the value of money is exactly what we make it. Based in our very early development as homo sapiens, humans need to feel that if they are giving something away, they are receiving something in return. The value of money is directly tied to this need to feel rewarded in some way.

On a surface level, money is valuable because we have developed a society in which we must use it to purchase goods. Money is essential because we need to exchange it for items that help us fulfill our basic needs. Shelter, food, and basic comforts cost money. These things also cost money to create. Therefore, there is a secondary need to pay those responsible for their creation. If our purchasing footprints were smaller, and we were able to buy the things we needed from local craftsmen and farmers, this money to labour equation would remain relatively simple. We would pay the person who made our food, shelter, or goods directly, based on the cost of the materials and labour. For example, we may pay a farmer for the cost of seeds, water, and his time spent in their field.

However, this becomes more complicated as we reintroduce human nature. We as humans place value on objects for a variety of reasons (rarity, for example), creating what we call perceived rarity. Modern day advertising and marketing tactics can target human nature, and make us desire things that are not truly valuable, but become “valuable” due to clever manipulation of our perception of rarity, productivity, or status. The financial value of an object rises if the perceived value rises. In summary, humans created a transactional system long ago that has persisted to this day in a variety of forms. However, due to more modern influences, our perception and understanding of value has changed, and so the value of money changes too. With higher value, comes a higher price tag.

Are GMOs Good, Bad, or Neither?

GMO food has been hotly contested for many years. Why would we genetically change our food, altering its natural state? The answer is not so simple. GMO crops can have extremely beneficial uses. For example, in countries with poor farming conditions, be it for long periods of drought or high populations of harmful insects, crops can be genetically modified to withstand those conditions. Likewise, genetically modified crops can have their nutritional value modified, and can give higher doses of vitamins and minerals to populations lacking a well rounded diet. When we compare this to more developed countries though, with the financial resources to give access to food that satisfies all of its population’s nutritional needs, we begin to see some questionable uses of genetic modification.

Growing crops that are the “best” version of themselves, selectively bred for colour, size, or beauty, can lead to mono-crop culture. This is when a single crop, or single type of crop is exclusively grown in one area. This leaves the crop vulnerable to disease or pests that can target a specific strain of crop. This is typically done to ensure that a competitor has more appealing product to win out over the competition at the grocery store.

However, GMO foods are often engineered to produce a much bigger crop, and allow better use of the already limited farmland that North American cities have to grow their local crops. If used responsibly, genetic modification of foods can have great benefit. It is up to the populations that choose to consume them to use their best judgement. If GMO food development was limited to scientists, researchers who could provide better agricultural opportunities to poverty stricken and food-poor countries, the question of if GMOs are more good than bad may be more easily answered.

The View From 2100

My view of the world in 2100 is bleak. I believe climate change will have become a reality, and only the richer countries will have implemented safeguards for its citizens. I believe the amount of animals and insects on earth will have dramatically decreased, and we will be facing more extreme weather events, with hotter summers and shorter and warmer winters.

Our only hope for the future is to start electing individuals who care about environmental policies, and the state of the climate crisis as it stands. We must support companies that actively work to minimize their environmental impact, and individuals who create technology to minimize our collective impact on the environment. I believe the days of young generations fictionalizing a shiny, futuristic, flying-car 2100 is over. Generation Z has been nicknamed the “doomers” because of their incredibly bleak outlook on the world’s climate, and the hope of making a change. We’ve entered a time of consciousness about the environmental impact humans have, but have also become aware that large companies that we as individuals cannot control are the source of the biggest climate damage.

It is large companies’ dependency on plastic, and disregard of environmental regulations that is doing a significant amount of damage to the climate. If we are to have any chance at a future that environmentally, looks similar to the world we live in now, we must act as individuals to create large scale change.
